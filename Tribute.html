Sir Isaac Newton

About

Sir Isaac Newton (25 December 1642 - 20 March 1726) was an English physicist and mathematician who is widely recognised as one of the most influential scientists of all time and as a key figure in the scientific revolution. His book Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica first published in 1687, laid the foundations for classical mechanics.

Newton's Principia formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, which dominated scientists view of the physical universe for the next three centuries. By deriving Kepler's laws of planetary motion from his mathematical description of gravity, and then using the same principles to account for the trajectories of comets, the tides, the precession of the equinoxes, and other phenomena. His work also demonstrated that the motion of the object on Earth and of celestial bodies could be described by the same principal.

Mechanics & Gravitation

Sir Isaac Newton returned to his work on mechanics by considering gravitation and its effects on the orbits of planets with reference to Kepler's laws of planetary motion. This followed stimultion by a brief exchange of letters in 1670-80 with Hooke, who had been appointed to manage the Royal Society's correspondance, and who opened a correspondance intended to elicit contributions from Newton to Royal Society transactions. Newton's reawakening interest in astronomical matters received further stimulus by the appearance of a comet in the winter of 1680-1681.

The Principia was published on 5th July 1687 with encouragement and financial from Edmond Halley. In his work, Newton stated the three universal law of motion. Together, these laws describe the relationship between any object, the forces acting upon it and the resulting motion, laying the foundation for classical mechanics.

Newton made clear that his heliocentric view of the Solar System developed in a somewhat modern way, because already in the mid-1680 he recognised the "deviation of the Sun" from the centre of gravity of Solar System. For Newton it was not precisely the centre of the Sun or any other body that could be considered at rest, but rather "the common centre of gravity of the Earth, the Sun and all the planets is to be esteem'd the centre of the world", and this centre gravity "either is at rest or moves uniformly forward in a right line.
